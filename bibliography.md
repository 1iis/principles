# Bibliography

Seminal ideas that we empirically validate or converge with.  
Great sources of inspiration in our experiments.

### Human augmentation

**Douglas Engelbart — [*Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework*](https://dougengelbart.org/AHI-Framework)**  
Engelbart framed computers as part of a larger human-tool system for improving comprehension, problem-solving, and coordinated work. The aim is not isolated cleverness, but increased capability in dealing with complex situations.

**J. C. R. Licklider — [*Man-Computer Symbiosis*](https://groups.csail.mit.edu/medg/people/psz/Licklider.html)**  
Licklider described a future of tightly coupled human-computer partnership: humans and machines doing together what neither does as well alone. The important word is not automation, but symbiosis.

**Alan Kay — [*The Computer Revolution Hasn’t Happened Yet*](https://archive.cra.org/Activities/grand.challenges/kay.pdf)**  
Kay treats computing less as a finished industry than as an unfinished medium. The computer is not merely a faster tool; it is a still-young environment for thought, learning, modeling, and expression.

**Bret Victor — [*Inventing on Principle*](https://worrydream.com/#!/InventingOnPrinciple), [*Learnable Programming*](https://worrydream.com/#!/LearnableProgramming)**  
Victor’s work asks what tools would look like if they were designed around human understanding: immediate feedback, visible behavior, explorable systems, and interfaces that help people think.

### Understanding

**Peter Naur — [*Programming as Theory Building*](https://gwern.net/doc/cs/algorithm/1985-naur.pdf)**  
Naur argues that programming is not chiefly the production of text, but the building of a theory in the programmer’s mind: an understanding of how the program relates to the world it serves. Source code matters, but it is not the whole knowledge.

**Fred Brooks — [*No Silver Bullet*](https://worrydream.com/refs/Brooks-NoSilverBullet.pdf)**  
Brooks distinguishes accidental complexity from essential complexity. Tools may reduce friction, but they do not remove the hard part: deciding what the system is, what it means, and how its pieces should fit.

**Edsger W. Dijkstra — [*The Humble Programmer*](https://www.cs.utexas.edu/~EWD/transcriptions/EWD03xx/EWD340.html)**  
Dijkstra’s recurring theme is intellectual discipline in the face of complexity. Programming demands humility because systems quickly exceed casual comprehension; correctness and clarity require deliberate restraint.

### Literate systems

**Donald Knuth — [*Literate Programming*](https://cs.stanford.edu/~knuth/lp.html)**  
Knuth’s literate programming turns the program into an explanation addressed to human beings, with executable code woven into the order of thought rather than merely the order demanded by the machine.

**Stephen Wolfram and Wolfram Notebooks — [*Wolfram Notebooks*](https://www.wolfram.com/notebooks/)**  
Wolfram’s notebooks helped establish the computational notebook as a technical medium: text, code, data, graphics, outputs, and interaction living together in one document. The notebook is not just a container for results, but a working surface for thought.

**Fernando Pérez, Brian Granger, and Project Jupyter — [*Project Jupyter*](https://jupyter.org/about), [*IPython history*](https://ipython.readthedocs.io/en/stable/about/history.html)**  
IPython began in 2001 as an interactive Python environment and grew into Jupyter in 2014: an open ecosystem for notebooks, kernels, rich outputs, and interactive computing across languages. Jupyter made the notebook a common medium for scientific computing, data work, teaching, and exploration.

**Jeremy Howard, fast.ai, Answer.AI — [*nbdev*](https://nbdev.fast.ai/), [*Solveit*](https://solve.it.com), [*Growing on Purpose*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SUZwYV5JYBM)**  
Howard’s work [@fastai](https://github.com/fastai) and [@AnswerDotAI](https://github.com/AnswerDotAI), on numerous projects like nbdev and Solveit, pushes notebooks further into software practice: write, test, document, package, explore, and now work with AI in the same medium. The broader idea is simple but deep: keep prose, code, data, outputs, tools, and intent close enough that understanding can compound.

### Composability

**Doug McIlroy and the Unix tradition — [*Unix Time-Sharing System Foreword*](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/j.1538-7305.1978.tb02135.x)**  
The Unix tradition prizes small programs, clear interfaces, and composition. A good tool does one thing well and combines naturally with other tools.

**David Parnas — [*On the Criteria To Be Used in Decomposing Systems into Modules*](https://doi.org/10.1145/361598.361623)**  
Parnas gave modularity a precise design criterion: hide decisions likely to change. Good modules are not just small; they protect the rest of the system from unnecessary knowledge.

**Brian Kernighan and Rob Pike — [*The Practice of Programming*](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/~bwk/tpop.webpage/)**  
Kernighan and Pike emphasize practical clarity: simple interfaces, readable code, incremental testing, debugging discipline, and the craft of making programs that can be understood and changed.

**Rich Hickey — [*Simple Made Easy*](https://www.infoq.com/presentations/Simple-Made-Easy/)**  
Hickey distinguishes simple from easy. Easy is near at hand; simple is not tangled. Simplicity is therefore not minimalism for its own sake, but the deliberate removal of unnecessary interdependence.

### Context discipline

As computers become networks of devices, files, accounts, models, tools, memories, agents, and capabilities, [context discipline](praxis/context-discipline.md) becomes the praxis behind [the new personal computer](telos/the-new-personal-computer.md): a context-shaped working environment organized around human intent.
