# Terms
> Disambiguation

We commonly denote the biological versus technological dichotomy by OPERATOR-MACHINE, or Human-Machine, or user-system.

The concept is that an OPERATOR is able to modify its environment (physical or virtual) through a MACHINE. The MACHINE cannot function without an OPERATOR to turn it on, and possibly to control its actions.

The new object usually called "AI" is both MACHINE and OPERATOR of machines. These terms have thus become ambiguous, because what they denote varies in context:

- Is the "OPERATOR" human or MACHINE? (both are possible: OPERATOR may be AI)
- Is the "MACHINE" being operated upon, or an OPERATOR itself? (both are also possible)

At a deeper level, these ambiguities denote fundamental category errors: the AI isn't a new *type* of computer but a new *feature*, a new *part* thereof; similar to the brain not being a *type* of human being but a feature, a part of thereof.

Finally, popular terms like "agent" or "assistant" worsen ambiguity in practice: they are both types of program and human roles. They also anthropomorphize the MACHINE, which adds confusion with the OPERATOR.

Rather than dilute the meaning of existing terminology by stretching its meaning too thin, we observe that the word *synthetic* seems to hint at the correct dichotomy. As opposed to human OPERATOR, we suggest using the term synthetic OPERATOR: **human** or **synth**, for short.

- We thus call **synth** a MACHINE with OPERATOR capability. This applies to computers, but also to robots, or any type of machine able to operate on its environment.
- We embrace the ambiguity and use MACHINE for all that is not human: both computer and AI. The opposite concept might be PERSON.
- Similarly, we use OPERATOR for all that controls a MACHINE: human or AI alike. The antonym might be ???

This *strongly* hints at the dual MACHINE-OPERATOR nature of synths.
