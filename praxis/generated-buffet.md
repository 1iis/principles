# Generated buffet
> Generate lots, keep very little

Cheap generation gives us a buffet. This is where [boring code][boring-is-good] and [context discipline][context-discipline] meet: generate enough to see, keep only enough to understand.

The point is the affordance of choice. We can ask for many implementations, names, outlines, interfaces, explanations, tests, or designs, then compare them directly. Generation turns imagination into visible options.

The useful act is selection. We apply our taste: we look for compactness, clarity, fit, boringness, and the absence of unnecessary abstraction. We keep the small part that improves the artifact and discard the rest.

This changes where thinking happens. We no longer have to manually produce every option before judging it. But judgment does not disappear; it moves to comparison, taste, and pruning.

Volume is suffocating when it becomes accumulation. More candidates should mean fewer, better survivors.

There is an evolutionary quality to the process: variation is cheap, selection is the work. The LLM produces candidates; the human applies pressure. Fitness means clarity, correctness, locality, maintainability, and low future cost.

Without selection, the buffet leads to indigestion, i.e. technical debt in polite circles, or [worse, for the human][slow-then-fast]. So we question every abstraction. We motivate every option. We prefer the compact solution that stays close to the data. The LLM should help us put less code into production than we could have written alone, not more.
